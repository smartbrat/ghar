Inside Bijoy Jain’s Alibaug retreat — where stone, water and quiet do the talking.

A coastal studio in Alibaug. A practice built by hand. And what happens when a master of slow architecture builds a house for himself.

Sumera Bhatia · Updated 12 June 2026 see editor’s note · 12 min read
J Presented by Jaquar Paid Partnership
Part 3 of 5 in Studio Visit

The short version

Bijoy Jain’s coastal house in Alibaug has been twelve years in the making2 and is still being built — by hand, from local materials, with no plan beyond what the family has learned by living there. It is the residential manifesto of slow architecture.

In this story 0 sections

    There is a road south of Bombay that nobody quite knows. It moves through coconut groves and small fishing villages, past low compound walls and roadside temples, and ends in a forest of casuarina pines where the air smells of salt and old wood. Bijoy Jain has lived at the end of this road for the better part of a decade — and built, by hand, the house he intends to die in.

    The house is not finished. The architect would say it never will be. Walls are added when needed and removed when they are not. Stone gets repurposed from one wing to another as the family’s life shifts around it. There is a swimming pool, then a year later there isn’t — the cement was cracking, so they let it become a garden. A coconut tree fell during the last monsoon and is now a bench. The house is more like a slow conversation with the land than a piece of architecture.

    The arrival

    You reach the gate by following a track that branches off the main road and immediately loses confidence in itself. Two stone pillars mark the entrance — unmarked, no signage — and beyond them is what looks at first like a clearing in the trees. Then you notice the verticals: lime-washed walls that hold the light differently, a roof line that breaks where a Banyan grew through it. The architecture is doing exactly what its architect is famous for: refusing to announce itself.

    Inside the gate, the practice that Bijoy Jain has spent thirty years refining is everywhere. Walls are made of stone laid without mortar in places — what the masons call “dry rubble”1 — and elsewhere of mud bricks that the family kneaded with their feet during the building. The roof tiles are reclaimed from a temple in Karnataka that was being torn down. Brass switches were cast by a local foundry from melted-down old door handles. Nothing here was bought from a catalogue.

    What got reused, salvaged, or remade

    • Temple roof tiles from a 19th-century Karnataka structure, reset by the same family of masons.
    • Brass switches and door pulls re-cast at a Mumbai foundry from melted door handles.
    • Teak beams pulled from a Konkan godown demolished for a road project.
    • A fallen coconut tree from the property, now the bench in the central courtyard.

    “The hand has its own intelligence. You can’t teach it from a screen.”

    Bijoy Jain

    A practice in slow motion

    Studio Mumbai, Jain’s practice, is the architectural world’s most unusual office. There are no computers on the desks — drawings are done in pencil on tracing paper, and revisions happen by laying a new sheet over the old. The studio sits in a Mumbai suburb behind an unmarked door, in a building Jain converted from a 1920s warehouse. Inside, the office is a workshop as much as a studio. Carpenters work on prototypes alongside architects. Stones brought back from sites sit in piles, waiting to be studied.

    The Alibaug house is, in many ways, the studio’s residential manifesto. Every material has a relationship with its maker. Every joint was tested in the workshop before it was made on site. Every room responds to where the sun is and where the breeze comes from — not where a plan said it should be.

    The house teaches you what it wants. You build a wall, you live with it for a year, you find out whether the wall is right. If it is wrong, the wall comes down. Architecture, for me, is just patience made visible.

    Bijoy Jain, in conversation with the author, Alibaug, March 2026

    What slow does that fast cannot

    The first thing you notice in the house — once your eyes adjust to the lower light — is that the proportions feel correct in a way that contemporary Indian architecture rarely manages. The ceilings are exactly where they should be. The doors are the right weight when you open them. The window seats invite you to sit. There is no trying to impress; the house is too comfortable in itself for that.

    This is what slow construction does that the industry’s standard timeline cannot. Each detail has been lived with, sometimes for years, before it was finalised. The kitchen counter was a temporary bench for two years; the family realised what height worked. The bedroom corridor was a covered porch for eighteen months; they realised they wanted it enclosed. The architecture is the result of habits, not drawings.

    Bijoy Jain Architect · Studio Mumbai

    “A house should learn its occupants — not the other way around.”

    The lessons

    For visitors — and there are many; architecture students travel from across the world to see this place — the lesson of the Alibaug house is not its style. The style is incidental. The lesson is the process. You cannot reverse-engineer this kind of architecture from the finished building. The materials, the proportions, the relationship to the site — they only happen if you give a project years of attention from people who know how to make things with their hands.

    This, Jain believes, is what is being lost in Indian architecture today. Not craftsmanship — there are still craftsmen — but the patience to use them. The patience for a wall to take six months to build. The patience to wait for the right stone. The patience to let a house become itself.

    Editor’s note

    Studio Mumbai does not currently take on residential commissions outside an ongoing waiting list. The Alibaug house is the practice’s private project — not a model to be replicated, and not open to the public.

    We leave at dusk. The forest is loud with crickets; the sky is purple over the casuarinas. Jain stands at the gate and watches the car go. The house behind him is older than when we arrived. Tomorrow they will start laying a new courtyard.

    The traditional approach

    Stone and lime

    Built by hand over months. Thermal mass cools interiors naturally. Repairable in place; ages without falling apart.

    The modern alternative

    Concrete block + foam

    Faster to build. Relies on mechanical cooling. Shorter lifespan; harder to maintain; produces more embodied carbon.
