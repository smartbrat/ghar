Photography Direction
Warm, natural light. Indian or relatable Asian faces when human subjects appear. Real interiors, real architecture — no stock-generic. Property photos use 4:3 aspect; heroes use 16:9; discovery grids use 1:1. When Pexels cannot provide confirmed Indian faces, use no-face alternatives: property aerials, city skylines, material close-ups.

Restraint

Shapes support.
They don't dominate.

The nine shapes are a vocabulary, not a wallpaper. Use them at roughly 5% of any page view — one per section, never all at once. Ghar.tv must look diverse, not monolithic.

Avoid — Overloaded

Homes worth talking about.

When shapes appear in every heading, every card, every corner, the brand becomes monotone. The shapes lose their power and the platform stops looking diverse.

Preferred — Restrained

Homes worth talking about.

One shape per section. Let whitespace, typography, and photography do the heavy lifting. The shape becomes a punctuation mark — not the sentence.

01
The 5% Rule
Shapes occupy roughly 5% of any given page view — the same discipline as brand red. They accent, not dominate.
02
One Per Section
A section can have one inline shape, OR a masked photo, OR an accent mark. Never all three. Pick the single most impactful use.
03
Diversity Over Identity
Ghar.tv must look like a diverse platform, not "the shapes brand." Content, typography, and photography carry the identity — shapes support it.

For developers and page builders

Rule 02 · Section Head

Every section
opens the same way.

Eyebrow on its own row at the top, then a 2-column row at desktop: title on the left, lead + CTA stacked on the right. Below 1024 it stacks vertically. The reading rhythm is identical across Intelligence, Editorial, GharTalks, Voices — that's the point. Every section uses the shared .sec-head / .sec-eyebrow / .sec-title / .sec-lead classes; section prefixes only layer max-width overrides on top.

Rule 03 · CTA Tiers

Two CTA tiers.
One per role.

Every CTA on the site belongs to one of two tiers — and they don't swap. Section main CTA is a bordered pill (.btn-text) — used once per section, next to the lead or under the description. Card-internal CTA is a text+arrow link (.btn-link) — used inside cards as a navigation cue because the card itself is already a click target. Putting a pill inside a card competes with the section CTA; putting a text link as the section CTA fails to anchor the section. One tier per role, never both.

Section · default Explore Services .btn-text — bordered pill, 1.5px ink border, 13/24 padding
Section · hover Explore Services Fills ink, arrow slides 3px right
Card · default Explore finance .btn-link — 13px ink, no border, no background
Card · hover Explore finance Color → brand-red, gap widens 6→9px, arrow slides 2px

Rejected variants — do not reintroduce

  • Pill on a card · competes with the section CTA. Cards are already click targets — use .btn-link for the inner cue.
  • Text link as section CTA · loses anchor weight. Section CTA needs the pill border to read as the section's primary call to act.
  • Filled brand-red buttons · red is reserved for page-level CTAs (Post Property, primary nav action) only.
  • Ghost / muted variants · both tiers stay ink-colored. No "Watch on YouTube" red, no muted-grey CTAs.
  • Custom paddings / sizes per section · tiers are canonical — .btn-text is 13/24, .btn-link is 13px text + 12px svg. No per-section tuning.

Rule 05 · Logo Discipline

The logo stands
alone — always.

A strict, non-negotiable rule. The Ghar.tv logo (the full red wordmark, the G letterform, or the mark + Suiza tagline lockup) is never paired with a shape glyph. The brand name “Ghar.tv” in a logo unit is never typed in Gazpacho, Inter, or any other font — it must use the canonical SVG logo asset. Gazpacho is fine when “Ghar.tv” appears inside a sentence or display title; it is not fine as a wordmark.

✕
Ghar.tv
House-shape glyph + Gazpacho text

Pairs a generic house glyph with a Gazpacho-rendered brand name. Neither piece is the actual logo. This pattern keeps appearing in footers, brand bars, and toast notifications — strip it on sight.

✕
Real logo + decorative shape glyph

Even when the actual logo is used, a shape glyph next to it is wrong. The logo carries its own identity. A shape next to it competes for the same job and reads as a second mark.

✓
Full Ghar.tv wordmark — the canonical logo

The real wordmark SVG. Use this in footers, navbars, and any moment that needs the full brand. Never replace the “Ghar.tv” characters with text styled in Gazpacho or Inter.

✓
G letterform — the standalone mark

Use the G letterform on its own when the full wordmark won't fit: app icons, favicons, social avatars, watermarks, copyright lines, notification toast icons. Never inside a colored badge or rounded square that mimics a logo lockup.

✓
A home worth talking about — that's what Ghar.tv is for.
“Ghar.tv” inside a sentence or title

When “Ghar.tv” appears inside running editorial copy, headlines, or display titles, it follows the surrounding font like any other word. Gazpacho is fine here — it's a sentence, not a logo unit.

Rule 06 · Whole-Card Link

The whole card
is the link.

When a card has one primary destination, wrap the entire card in <a> so the full surface is clickable. The "Explore X →" cue at the bottom stays for affordance but becomes a <span> — nested anchors are invalid HTML. Card hover propagates to the inner cue so the link visual updates from anywhere on the surface.

✓ Card is the anchor; inner cue is a span

Entire surface is the click target. The bottom “Explore X →” reads as a link cue, not a separate link.

<!-- The whole card is one anchor. -->
<a href="/services/finance" class="sv4-card sv4-card--finance">
  <div class="sv4-card-ico">…</div>
  <h3 class="sv4-card-name">Ghar Finance</h3>
  <p  class="sv4-card-desc">…</p>
  <!-- inner cue is a span, NOT an a -->
  <span class="btn-link sv4-card-cta">Explore finance →</span>
</a>
✗ Card is article, inner CTA is the anchor

Only the small “Explore X” text is clickable. Users who tap the card surface, image, or title get no response.

<!-- Click area limited to the inner text link. -->
<article class="sv4-card">
  <div class="sv4-card-ico">…</div>
  <h3 class="sv4-card-name">Ghar Finance</h3>
  <p  class="sv4-card-desc">…</p>
  <a href="/services/finance" class="btn-link">Explore →</a>
</article>

Required when the whole card is a link

  • Reset anchor styles on the card: text-decoration: none; color: inherit;
  • Focus-visible outline so keyboard users see card focus: outline: 2px solid var(--ink); outline-offset: 4px;
  • Propagate card hover to inner cue so the link visual reads from anywhere on the surface: .xx-card:hover .xx-card-cta { gap: 9px; } and .xx-card:hover .xx-card-cta svg { transform: translateX(2px); } — color stays ink, the hover cue is motion only (no brand-red flip on text links)
  • Inner cue is .btn-link per Rule 03 — text+arrow, no border, because the card itself carries the click affordance
Rule 07 · Carousel Furniture

Furniture appears
only in carousel mode.

The carousel chassis (.rail-outer / .rail + initCarousel) is shared across every rail-using section. But the visible furniture — arrow buttons, cursor: grab, bleed-edge margins — appears ONLY at the breakpoint where the row overflows. On the grid breakpoint where cards fit, the chassis is silent. Disabled arrows on a grid that doesn't scroll read as broken UX. A grab cursor on a static row is misleading.

≥ 1024 px Grid mode · silent chassis

All 3 cards fit. No arrows, no bleed, no grab cursor.

Rejected variants — do not reintroduce

  • Arrows always visible (disabled on desktop) · reads as broken UX. If there's nothing to navigate, don't show the control.
  • Grab cursor on a non-overflowing grid · the cursor promises drag; the grid can't deliver. Misleading.
  • Bleed-edge margins on a grid layout · bleed exists so the next card peeks past the viewport edge. With no overflow, the “peek” never appears — just an awkward left/right gap.
  • Separate “always rail” chassis to dodge the rule · some sections naturally always overflow (Architecture & Design has 7 cards). That's the same rule applied to a section permanently in carousel mode — not an exception.

06 — The rules for scaling the voice

Eight principles.
If a decision breaks any one of them, the decision is wrong.

These aren't style guidelines. They're the rules that keep the voice coherent across sixteen sections, six card types, B2C and B2B audiences, and years of future content. When you're three screens deep into a layout decision and can't choose — come back here.

01
Warm paper is the canvas.
Body is #faf7f2 everywhere — product surfaces too. Pure white is a deliberate object that floats on the paper (inner cards, search bars, pills). If a section needs pure white, it's wrong.
02
The house is a glyph.
The nine canonical shapes are typography. They live inside sentences, prefix categories, open quotes, anchor data. Never decoration. Never icons. If a shape isn't doing the work of a letter, cut it.
03
Color is vocabulary — earned, not assigned.
Indigo Mist for Editorial (written ink), Espresso for GharTalks (listening room), Turmeric for GharEvents (ceremony), Ink for VideoWorks (cinema), Terracotta for Community (earth of people). Five earned tones — none repeated, none adjacent. If you can't defend a color choice semantically in one sentence, and the section next to it isn't warm paper, it doesn't belong.
04
Stories, not specs.
Write every line for the person reading, not the business speaking. "5 new listings match your filter" becomes "a new home story starts with five matches." This is not word-play — it's stance.
05
The product is a polite guest.
Search bars sit next to fireplaces. Notifications speak softly. The interface participates in the room — it doesn't yank you out of it. If a component interrupts the mood, rewrite the component.
06
Data is typography.
Big serif stats. Shape-silhouettes as data dots. Histograms, not rainbow pie charts. Intelligence without clinical. Assume the reader can read a chart — don't dumb the data down to marketing.
07
B2B earns authority through craft.
Broker, developer, and partner sections keep the warm voice but swap emotion for precision. Sharper type hierarchy, tighter grids, denser proof. Never colder color — just tighter composition. Atmosphere stays; editorial shifts.
08
One voice. Sixteen rooms.
Variety lives within the voice, not against it. Different sections have different weight, density, color, and rhythm — but the reader should never feel they left Ghar.tv. If a section feels like it belongs to a different brand, it does. Rebuild it.
A homepage isn't a screen. It's an opening chapter.
Build every section as if the reader will stop here and read to the end.

UX Principles.

For product, design, and developers

How interactions should feel — calm, mobile-first, intuitive, trustworthy. Touch targets, scroll behavior, overlays, loading states, errors, accessibility. The platform takes Airbnb as primary reference; storytelling sections layer in Apple / Squarespace / Linear / Stripe motion craft, never breaking the calm.

Coming next pass

What will live here

The UX chapter will document how Ghar.tv interactions feel — the implicit decisions that make the platform read as “calm and intuitive” instead of busy. Expect short principles backed by concrete examples drawn from index4 patterns.

  • Philosophy — Understanding-first, not transaction-first. Calm over loud. Editorial over salesy. Discovery-driven browsing.
  • Mobile-first — Default styles target ≤743px; desktop layers on. No feature on desktop hidden or broken on mobile.
  • Touch & Gesture — Touch targets minimum 44px height. touch-action: pan-y + 8px direction-detection threshold before capturing horizontal swipe on carousels.
  • Scroll & Overlays — Off-canvas / modals block scroll via wheel/touchmove event prevention, NOT position:fixed on body. overscroll-behavior: contain on scrollable panels. Each panel its own scroll container.
  • Loading States — Skeleton patterns for cards/rails, inline spinners for buttons, shimmer for hero placeholders. Never spin-on-empty.
  • Error States — Inline form validation, toast for global errors, empty-state cards with a clear next action. No raw error codes shown to users.
  • Accessibility — Every interactive element has hover, focus-visible, and active states. Keyboard navigation. AA contrast minimum. Screen-reader semantics.
